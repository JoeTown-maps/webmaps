The Robidoux Hill Historic District

This district epitomizes the 19th and early 20th century aspiring middle class.

Links and Documents

Original Application (saved on MO State Parks site)

Lying on the hilltops overlooking the commercial heart of the city, the district epitomizes the pattern of aspiring middle class housing. The majority of the houses where built as Victorian single-family residences intermixed with late ninetheenth century duplexes. The district also features examples of Second-Empire, Italianate, Queen Anne (and variants), and venacular homes. A number of residences feature carriage houses. The majority of these are constructed of brick and mimic the style of the period. Some modern structures have been built and at least one commercial building of the era survives.

The distinctiveness of the district lies primarily in the retention of the physical characteristics associated with Victorian era family housing.

Select Houses and Buildings from the original application

4th Street
614 N. 4th

A single-story brick Italianate house built ca. 1880 for Walter Sanders. It features a truncated hip roof with boxed cornices and has a two bay façade. The first bay is dominated by a chamfer-shaped bay window with segmental arched window bays in all three facets. The original porch has been removed.

5th Street
517 N. 5th

A Second Empire house built by John S. Lemon in two stages. The first, built in 1871 is the Second Empire façade with a slate covered mansard roof with elaborate dormer windows and a bracketed and dentiled frieze. The second story windows have elaborate bracketed window hoods and segmental arched openings. The first floor windows feature half-round windows and fanciful bracketed hoods. A centered projecting wing in the symmetrical façade houses the front entrance. A porch with modillioned cornice and chamfered posts fills the southwest corner. The second stage is a wing at the rear of the house in 1889. This addition picks up the details and rhythms of the first stage and features several oustanding stained glass windows.

603 N. 5th

This is incorrectly listed in the application as 605 N. 5th

A two-and-one-half story brick Queen Anne/Stick style house built ca. 1885 by Nelson Riley. It is irregularly massed with a projecting rectangle wing on the facade featuring a shingled gable supported on consoles. Projecting from the face of the wing is a two-story bay with triple windows on both stories. The exposed half-timber work visible in the gables and on the open facade porch is reminiscent of a variant of the Queen Anne style called Stick style. A projected bay on the south elevation with a centerted chimney is also found on the rear of the house.

At the alley behind the house is a one-and-one-half story brick carriage house and attached frame barn. There is a single entry door in the gable end facing Isadore street with a hoymow door above.

6th Street
524 N. 6th

A two-story Greek Revival house built ca. 1865 for local merchant Joseph Inslee. It is retangular in shape with a low hip roof and a symmetrical three-bay façade with triangular shaped and moulded stone lintels. The porch was added ca. 1915. It fills the façade and is supported by tapering wood posts resting on brick piers.

608 N. 6th

A rectangular two-story brick Italianate house, this was built as a twin to 602 N. 6th. A flat roof lies bhind the façade parapet with three symmetrical bays at either story. The façade openings have raised brick surrounds feature keystone accents.
